Medical Collaborator Interview Guide

Use this page during the meeting to align medical terminology, population scope, search strategy, support strategy taxonomy, screening criteria, and ethics for the wearable AI accessibility scoping review.

Open Questions Record Audio Review Search Protocol

Meeting Goal

By the end of this conversation, we want enough medical input to produce the next version of the scoping review method: population terms, medical keywords, support strategy taxonomy, inclusion / exclusion logic, and any safety or non-pathologizing language concerns.

Recording note: Ask for permission before recording. The recorder on this page saves audio in the browser for download; it does not automatically upload the recording.

Suggested 45-60 Minute Agenda

  1. Project framing: explain the health-to-HCI translation goal.
  2. Population scope: confirm which groups and conditions belong in the review.
  3. Medical terminology: align clinical / rehab terms with HCI terms.
  4. Search strategy: review the seven search layers and database choices.
  5. Data extraction: decide what medical information must be coded.
  6. Ethics and risk: identify language, inference, and privacy concerns.
  7. Outputs: ask for seed papers, terms to add, and terms to avoid.

Question List

1. Scope and Populations

  1. From a medical or health perspective, which populations are most relevant to this review?
  2. Are autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, sensory processing differences, anxiety, low vision, hearing differences, and vestibular sensitivity reasonable entry points?
  3. What important groups are missing, such as intellectual disability, learning disability, traumatic brain injury, mild cognitive impairment, dementia, aphasia, migraine, photosensitive epilepsy, PTSD, chronic fatigue, or chronic pain?
  4. Which diagnosis terms should be treated carefully because they may broaden the review too much?
  5. Should the final taxonomy be organized by diagnosis, by access need, or by support strategy?

2. Medical Keywords and Terminology

  1. In medical, rehab, or occupational therapy literature, what terms describe sound, color, brightness, vibration, frequency, timing, and stimulus intensity?
  2. Which terms are most standard: sensory modulation, sensory regulation, sensory processing, sensory overload, stimulus control, sensory diet, or environmental adaptation?
  3. What are the best terms for executive function support, attention support, task sequencing, memory support, and cognitive scaffolding?
  4. How should we search for motor fatigue, gesture fatigue, upper-limb fatigue, posture, tremor, balance, and low-effort action?
  5. Which MeSH terms, subject headings, or database-specific terms should we add for PubMed, PsycINFO, CINAHL, or Embase?

3. Support Strategies

  1. What support strategies are common for neurodivergent or sensory-sensitive users?
  2. Which strategies are about modifying the stimulus itself, such as sound, color, brightness, vibration, flicker, or frequency?
  3. Which strategies are about structure and cognition, such as prompts, reminders, task chunking, planning, and external organization?
  4. Which strategies are about environment, such as noise reduction, lighting adjustment, preparation, predictability, or safe space?
  5. Which support strategies require clinician, caregiver, teacher, or human-in-the-loop involvement?
  6. Which strategies should not be automatically translated into AI adaptation without user control?

4. Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

  1. If a paper only discusses diagnosis but no support, accommodation, intervention, accessibility, or design implication, should we exclude it?
  2. If a paper is health-only and does not mention wearable or digital technology, when should it still be included as evidence for health-to-HCI translation?
  3. How much clinical intervention literature should be included before the review becomes too broad for CHI?
  4. Which clinical study types are most useful for this review: trials, reviews, qualitative studies, clinical guidelines, case studies, occupational therapy papers, or assistive technology papers?
  5. Should we rate evidence strength, or only map evidence type because this is a scoping review?

5. Data Extraction and Coding

  1. What fields should we extract from medical papers beyond diagnosis and sample size?
  2. Should support strategies be coded as sensory modulation, cognitive scaffolding, attention management, motor accommodation, environmental adaptation, multimodal substitution, personalization, or human-in-the-loop support?
  3. What outcomes should be recorded: anxiety reduction, overload reduction, task completion, fatigue reduction, autonomy, safety, quality of life, or adherence?
  4. How should we code the difference between clinical needs, lived experience needs, and technology design implications?
  5. What would make a finding strong enough to influence a wearable AI accessibility principle?

6. AI, Risk, and Ethics

  1. What risks arise if wearable AI infers attention, fatigue, stress, emotion, cognitive load, intent, or ability from gaze, movement, heart rate, skin conductance, EEG, or behavior?
  2. What neurodivergent behaviors should not be treated as deficits or abnormal signals by AI?
  3. Which data types are most sensitive from a medical or ethical perspective?
  4. How should we describe non-pathologizing design in language that medical and HCI audiences both accept?
  5. What user controls are medically or ethically important: opt-out, override, correction, consent, clinician configuration, local processing, or confidence display?

7. Concrete Outputs We Need From This Meeting

  1. A revised population list.
  2. A medical keyword replacement table.
  3. A list of must-use MeSH / subject heading terms.
  4. A support strategy taxonomy from the health side.
  5. Seed papers, review papers, guidelines, or known authors.
  6. Terms to avoid because they are outdated, stigmatizing, or clinically inaccurate.
  7. Recommendations for revising the seven-layer search protocol.
