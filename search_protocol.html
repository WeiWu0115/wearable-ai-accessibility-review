Protocol Positioning

This protocol is designed for a CHI full paper level scoping review. The search is intentionally layered because the project does not simply ask whether wearable accessibility systems exist. It asks how health, rehabilitation, and neurodiversity support knowledge can be translated into HCI and wearable AI accessibility principles.

Search logic: Do not force every health paper to mention wearable technology. First capture clinically grounded support strategies, then map them to HCI, XR, wearable computing, and AI-mediated interaction.

Review Questions Covered by the Search

  1. What cognitive, sensory, motor, perceptual, emotional, environmental, and AI-mediated access needs are relevant to wearable intelligence?
  2. What health, rehabilitation, occupational therapy, education, and assistive technology strategies support these needs?
  3. How have HCI, XR, wearable computing, accessible computing, and human-AI interaction translated these needs into design principles or system features?
  4. What principles are needed for wearable AI systems that sense, infer, personalize, and adapt in real-world embodied contexts?

Seven Search Layers

Each layer should be run separately and logged with database, date, exact query, filters, result count, and export count. Search strings should be adapted to each database syntax.

Layer 1: Health-Only Support Strategies

Purpose: Capture clinically grounded support strategies without requiring the paper to mention technology.

("sensory modulation" OR "sensory regulation" OR "cognitive rehabilitation" OR "cognitive scaffolding" OR "executive function support" OR "attention support" OR "fatigue management" OR "environmental adaptation")
AND
(autism OR autistic OR ADHD OR neurodivergent OR dyslexia OR dyspraxia OR "cognitive impairment" OR "mild cognitive impairment" OR dementia OR anxiety OR "low vision" OR blindness OR "hearing impairment")

Layer 2: Neurodiversity and Sensory Modulation

Purpose: Capture sound, color, light, vibration, frequency, intensity, predictability, and sensory profile literature.

("sensory overload" OR "sensory modulation" OR "sensory processing" OR "sensory sensitivity" OR "sensory profile" OR "sensory diet" OR "stimulus control")
AND
(autism OR autistic OR neurodivergent OR ADHD OR anxiety OR migraine OR photosensitivity)
AND
(sound OR audio OR noise OR color OR light OR brightness OR haptic OR vibration OR flicker OR flashing OR frequency OR intensity OR predictability)

Layer 3: Cognitive Support and Executive Function

Purpose: Capture attention, working memory, task sequencing, prompt design, and cognitive load support.

("executive function" OR attention OR "working memory" OR "cognitive load" OR "mental workload" OR confusion OR comprehension OR "task sequencing" OR "task management")
AND
(support OR intervention OR accommodation OR "assistive technology" OR prompting OR reminders OR scaffolding OR "external structure")
AND
(ADHD OR autism OR autistic OR neurodivergent OR "cognitive impairment" OR aging OR dementia OR "mild cognitive impairment")

Layer 4: Motor Fatigue and Embodied Interaction

Purpose: Capture gesture burden, posture, upper-limb fatigue, tremor, balance, and alternative input.

("motor accessibility" OR "motor impairment" OR "motor fatigue" OR "upper-limb fatigue" OR tremor OR posture OR balance OR reach OR "gesture fatigue")
AND
(wearable OR "smart glasses" OR "augmented reality" OR "virtual reality" OR XR OR "gesture interaction" OR "gaze input" OR "voice input" OR "alternative input" OR "switch access")

Layer 5: Perceptual and Environmental Accessibility

Purpose: Capture low vision, blindness, hearing access, spatial awareness, public environments, lighting, and noise.

("low vision" OR blindness OR "visual impairment" OR deaf OR "hard of hearing" OR "hearing impairment" OR "vestibular sensitivity" OR "spatial awareness" OR "environmental awareness")
AND
(accessibility OR "assistive technology" OR navigation OR captions OR "spatial audio" OR haptic OR "object recognition" OR "environmental adaptation")
AND
(wearable OR "smart glasses" OR "augmented reality" OR XR OR mobile OR "digital health")

Layer 6: HCI, XR, and Wearable Accessibility

Purpose: Capture design principles, interaction techniques, accessible computing, and HCI evaluations.

(accessibility OR "accessible computing" OR "inclusive design" OR "universal design" OR "cognitive accessibility" OR neurodiversity OR disability)
AND
("human-computer interaction" OR HCI OR "augmented reality" OR "virtual reality" OR XR OR "wearable interaction" OR "smart glasses" OR "human-AI interaction")
AND
(guideline OR framework OR principle OR taxonomy OR "design implication" OR "design recommendation" OR evaluation OR "participatory design")

Layer 7: Human-AI Accessibility and Inference Risk

Purpose: Capture adaptation, personalization, cognitive-state inference, explainability, user control, privacy, and misclassification.

("artificial intelligence" OR AI OR "machine learning" OR "adaptive system" OR "context-aware AI" OR "human-AI interaction" OR "affect recognition" OR "intent recognition" OR "cognitive state inference" OR "behavioral sensing" OR biosignal)
AND
(accessibility OR disability OR neurodiversity OR "cognitive accessibility")
AND
(explainability OR transparency OR fairness OR personalization OR autonomy OR agency OR "user control" OR override OR contestability OR privacy OR misclassification OR uncertainty)

Screening and Calibration Plan

  1. Run a pilot search in PubMed, ACM Digital Library, Scopus, and IEEE Xplore.
  2. Sample 20 to 50 records per layer to check whether each layer is too broad, too narrow, or missing known seed papers.
  3. Two reviewers independently screen the first 100 to 300 titles and abstracts.
  4. Resolve disagreements and refine inclusion / exclusion criteria before full screening.
  5. Record reasons for exclusion using stable codes: no accessibility relevance, no health or support relevance, no HCI or wearable relevance, purely technical, purely clinical diagnosis, no human-centered contribution, duplicate, full text unavailable.
  6. Use backward and forward citation chaining for seed papers and highly relevant reviews.

Why This Is Stronger Than a Basic Search

  • It separates health-only support knowledge from technology-specific HCI literature, reducing premature technology bias.
  • It uses both database search and venue hand-search, which is important for CHI, ASSETS, IMWUT, XR, and wearable computing coverage.
  • It includes controlled vocabulary and free-text terms, improving coverage across medical and computing databases.
  • It explicitly covers AI inference risk, which is central to wearable intelligence and distinct from screen-based accessibility.
  • It supports PRISMA-ScR reporting through layer-level logging, screening calibration, exclusion codes, and citation chaining.
